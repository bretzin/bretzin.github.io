What They Do

CapturePoint is a full-stack carbon infrastructure company — owning and operating pipelines, injection wells, and underground storage rights. They build dedicated CO₂ capture equipment directly at industrial client sites, connect it to their own pipeline network, and inject the CO₂ either into oil reservoirs for enhanced recovery or into deep saline formations for permanent sequestration.

Think of them as the carbon management utility for industrial facilities — the same way an industrial gas company (Air Products, Air Liquide) builds and operates a nitrogen plant on a client's site and sells the output under a long-term supply agreement.

Revenue Streams

Stream 01
CO₂ Offtake Fees

Industrial clients pay CapturePoint to accept and dispose of their CO₂ under long-term contracted agreements. Take-or-pay provisions protect revenue even during client outages.

Stream 02
Oil Production (EOR)

CO₂ injected into mature oil reservoirs mobilizes residual oil. CapturePoint earns oil revenue from their operated EOR units — the Oklahoma Hub and Panhandle Units.

Stream 03
45Q Tax Credits

Up to $85/metric ton for permanently sequestered CO₂ under current US tax law. A major revenue driver for the CENLA Hub and future sequestration-focused projects.

Stream 04
Carbon Market Credits

Verified sequestration generates carbon credits tradeable in voluntary and compliance markets. Value varies with market pricing but provides additional revenue on top of 45Q.

Competitive Moat

Pipeline connections create switching costs that are nearly prohibitive. Once CapturePoint has physically tied their pipeline into a client's facility, the client would need to fund new pipeline construction, negotiate new storage rights, and obtain new regulatory permits — a process taking years and costing tens of millions — to switch providers.
Brownfield vs. Greenfield Strategy

Brownfield (existing assets): Oklahoma Carbon Hub and Panhandle Units were acquired from conventional oil and gas operators who built them for EOR without the carbon management angle. CapturePoint acquired these at oil-field valuations and is monetizing them at carbon-infrastructure valuations — a meaningful arbitrage. These assets generate current cash flow to fund greenfield development.

Greenfield (new development): CENLA Hub in Louisiana is the flagship — new wells, new pipelines, new client connections built from scratch. Exploratory acreage in Colorado, Wyoming, and New Mexico represents early-stage options on future hubs. Higher risk but where step-change value creation lives.

Key Commercial Risk

All infrastructure is sized to contracted client volumes. CapturePoint's entire capital investment depends on clients running their facilities reliably at agreed rates. Multi-client hubs like CENLA mitigate this — if one client has an outage, the hub infrastructure continues to be utilized by others.
Section 02 — Execution
Project Phases
End-to-end project lifecycle for a greenfield CO₂ capture, pipeline, and sequestration facility.
1
Development & Sanction
  • Commission independent geological study; drill characterization well(s); build reservoir simulation model
  • Negotiate and execute CO₂ offtake agreements — volume, pressure, quality specs, pricing, term, take-or-pay provisions
  • Secure surface land rights, pipeline easements, and deep pore space storage rights
  • File EPA Class VI injection well permit applications (budget 2–4 years for approval)
  • Obtain state pipeline permits, air permits, environmental impact assessments, PHMSA registration
  • Execute FEED (Front End Engineering & Design) — PFDs, P&IDs, equipment sizing, hydraulic model, ±15% cost estimate
  • Present to board/investors for capital sanction; secure project financing
  • Establish project controls baseline — budget, schedule, scope
2
Detailed Engineering
  • Final P&IDs, detailed equipment specifications and datasheets
  • Piping specifications — material selection critical for CO₂ service (corrosion allowances, wet CO₂ metallurgy)
  • Structural and civil design — foundations, buildings, pipe racks
  • Electrical, instrumentation, and control system architecture design
  • Pipeline alignment sheets and water/road crossing designs
  • HAZOP (Hazard & Operability) review and Safety Instrumented System (SIS) design
  • Conduct LNTP (Limited Notice to Proceed) to compressor vendor to secure manufacturing slot — must happen immediately after sanction
3
Procurement
  • Issue purchase orders for long lead equipment — compressors first (see Equipment tab)
  • Procure capture system skids (amine absorption, dehydration units)
  • Line pipe procurement — API 5L carbon steel, FBE coating, cathodic protection components
  • Wellhead christmas trees, downhole tubing, packer assemblies rated for CO₂ service
  • DCS/SCADA control system with Factory Acceptance Testing (FAT)
  • Power transformers and electrical equipment (often overlooked — critical path risk)
  • Fire and gas detection systems, SCBA stations, emergency shutdown panels
  • Execute Master Service Agreements with preferred EPC contractors
4
Construction
  • Pipeline: clear ROW, trench, string pipe, bend, weld, NDT inspection (radiography/UT), coat, lower, backfill, restore
  • Road and waterway crossings — bored or open cut per permit
  • Hydrostatic pressure test at 1.25× MAOP before commissioning
  • Install cathodic protection, block valves, pig launchers/receivers, markers
  • Drill injection wells — set and cement multiple casing strings through all freshwater zones
  • Perforate casing, run packer and injection tubing string, install wellhead and christmas tree
  • Facility civil earthwork, foundations, structural steel, equipment setting, piping, electrical, instrumentation
  • Capture facility construction at client site — coordinate tie-in outage windows with client
  • Pre-commissioning punch list completion, system cleaning and flushing
5
Commissioning & Startup
  • Instrument calibration — all flow meters, pressure transmitters, temperature elements
  • SCADA loop checks and functional logic testing
  • Safety system testing — ESD logic, fire and gas response, pressure relief verification
  • Compressor mechanical run test
  • Pipeline commissioning — nitrogen purge then introduce CO₂
  • Well commissioning — initial injection at low rate, step up to confirm injectivity matches model
  • Performance testing against design basis
  • Regulatory notifications for first injection; handover to operations with as-built drawings and manuals
6
Operations
  • Continuous SCADA monitoring of pressures, flows, temperatures across entire system
  • Preventive maintenance program for compressors, valves, instruments
  • Pipeline integrity management — periodic inline inspection (pigging), CP surveys, aerial patrols
  • Annual wellbore mechanical integrity tests (MIT) per EPA Class VI requirements
  • Corrosion monitoring — coupons, inspection points, chemical injection optimization
  • Quarterly and annual regulatory reporting to EPA on injection volumes, pressures, plume data
  • Reservoir model updates as actual injection data accumulates
  • Groundwater monitoring from dedicated monitoring wells

Section 04 — Engineering Basis
Process Conditions
The governing variable and the cascade of process conditions derived from it that define every equipment selection decision.
The governing variable is contracted CO₂ throughput capacity — expressed in metric tons per year (mtCO₂/yr), converted to MMscfd for equipment sizing. Everything else flows from this number, which is itself driven by what the client's facility actually produces.

Client CO₂ Production — The True Foundation

Before any engineering begins, the client's process must be fully characterized. Four factors determine the actual capturable volume:

  • Gross CO₂ production — what the process chemistry and plant throughput dictate (theoretical maximum)
  • Capturable fraction — concentration matters enormously. Near-pure streams (ethanol fermentation, fertilizer synthesis) are far cheaper to capture than dilute flue gas (10–15% CO₂ from combustion). High concentration = better economics.
  • Capture efficiency — a well-designed amine system captures 85–95% of the CO₂ in the feed stream. Higher efficiency costs more.
  • Client operational reliability — actual utilization rate (typically 85–92% for industrial plants), planned shutdowns, feedstock variability. This directly drives real revenue vs. contracted revenue.

Process Conditions — Design Cascade

01 Design Flow Rate ▾
Start with contracted volume in mtCO₂/yr → convert to volumetric flow (MMscfd) using CO₂ molecular weight → apply 10–20% design margin for peak flows and future growth → establish turndown rate (typically 50–70% of design rate). This is the anchor number every piece of equipment is sized against.
02 CO₂ Stream Composition & Quality ▾
Key contaminants that affect everything downstream: Water (drives dehydration system — critical for corrosion and hydrate prevention), H₂S (changes regulatory classification and materials), N₂ / CH₄ / O₂ (affect compression thermodynamics), Particulates (drive inlet filtration). Pipeline and injection specs define maximum allowable limits. CO₂ purity of 95%+ typically required.
03 Suction Pressure (Compressor Inlet) ▾
What pressure is CO₂ available at from the client's process? Amine capture systems strip CO₂ at near-atmospheric pressure. Lower suction pressure = higher overall compression ratio = more stages and larger driver. This is one of the two critical compressor sizing inputs.
04 Pipeline Discharge Pressure ▾
Driven by pipeline length and diameter, elevation changes, and minimum delivery pressure at the injection wellhead. CO₂ pipelines must keep CO₂ in dense phase (supercritical) throughout — requiring minimum ~1,100 PSI. Typical operating pressures: 1,200–2,200 PSI. Together with suction pressure, this defines the total compression ratio — the other critical compressor sizing input.
05 Temperature Conditions ▾
Ambient temperature range drives cooling system design and winterization. Maximum allowable CO₂ temperature entering pipeline typically limited to 120°F to protect pipe coating and manage thermodynamic behavior. Minimum temperature considerations — CO₂ expanding across pressure drops can get very cold, risking dry ice formation or equipment damage.
06 Pipeline Hydraulic Model ▾
With flow rate, pressures, route, and elevation profile established, a hydraulic simulation determines: optimal pipe diameter (capital vs. operating cost tradeoff), whether intermediate booster compression is needed, pressure profile confirming dense phase is maintained throughout, and pig launcher/receiver placement for future inline inspection.
07 Injection Well Requirements ▾
Reservoir injectivity data from characterization wells defines how many metric tons per day each well can accept. Divide total volume by per-well capacity to get well count. Wellhead injection pressure must overcome reservoir pressure plus hydrostatic head — feeds back into pipeline discharge pressure requirements.
08 Power Requirements ▾
Once compressor sizing is established, total installed horsepower is known. Drives substation sizing, backup generator sizing, fuel gas supply requirements. Compression power is typically the largest ongoing operating expense of the facility.
The Basis of Design document is the living document capturing all agreed process conditions. Any change after formal approval must go through a Management of Change (MOC) process with documented cost, schedule, and safety impact assessment. Getting this frozen early is the highest-leverage thing a PM can do to protect budget and schedule.

Key Inputs Required for Vendor Sizing

  • Actual inlet flow rate (MMscfd at suction conditions)
  • Suction pressure (PSI) and suction temperature (°F)
  • Discharge pressure (PSI)
  • Gas composition (mol% of each component)
  • Available driver type (electric motor, gas engine, or gas turbine)
  • Turndown requirement (minimum operating flow %)
  • Desired number of compression stages
  • Spare philosophy (N+1 redundancy or shared spare)

Typical Outcome for CapturePoint Projects

Small / Medium Facility
Reciprocating Preferred

Single industrial emitter, 200K–750K mtCO₂/yr. Lower flow rates, high pressure ratios, variable feed rates, and remote locations all favor reciprocating. Typically 2–3 units for redundancy.

Large Hub Facility
Centrifugal Attractive

Multiple emitters feeding a central hub, millions of mtCO₂/yr. High sustained flow, stable aggregated composition, and grid-connected power shift the balance toward centrifugal.

Phasing consideration: Hubs like CENLA will start small and grow as more emitters connect. Reciprocating compressors are more modular — you can add a unit as volumes grow without redesigning the system. This often pushes early-stage hub projects toward reciprocating even if centrifugal is the long-term answer at full build-out.
