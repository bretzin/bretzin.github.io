What Makes Data Centers Different

Data centers are among the most capital-intensive and schedule-sensitive infrastructure projects in the current market. Hyperscale tenants sign leases contingent on specific power delivery dates — missing a commercial operation date (COD) means losing the customer entirely, not just a delay penalty. The schedule pressure is existential, not financial.

Power is the governing constraint. Unlike a traditional industrial project where long lead equipment drives the schedule, data center development is fundamentally about securing power — getting sufficient grid capacity contracted, interconnected, and delivered to the site before anything else can proceed at scale.

Market Context

Demand Driver
AI & HPC Growth

AI model training and inference workloads are driving unprecedented demand for compute-dense, high-power data center capacity. GPU clusters require 10–30x the power density of traditional server deployments.

Supply Constraint
Grid Capacity

In most major markets, utility grid capacity is fully subscribed 2–5 years out. Interconnection queue wait times have grown from months to years. Power availability now determines where data centers can be built.

Revenue Model
Long-Term Leases

Hyperscale customers (cloud providers) sign 10–20 year leases for shell space, power, and cooling. Colocation operators lease individual racks or cages. Both models are contracted, recurring revenue businesses.

Capital Requirement
$10M–$20M+ per MW

All-in development cost for a hyperscale campus ranges widely by market, power cost, and density but $10M–$20M per MW of IT load capacity is a reasonable planning range for Tier III/IV facilities.

Power comes first. Before committing to land acquisition, confirm that sufficient grid power can be secured in the target location and timeline. A site with cheap land and no power is worthless. A site with expensive land and firm 200MW of power availability is invaluable.
Section 02 — Development
Site Development
Site selection, land acquisition, entitlement, zoning, and permitting framework for data center development.

Site Selection Criteria

Critical
Power Availability

Confirmed available grid capacity from the serving utility. Transmission access, substation proximity, and interconnection queue position. On-site generation potential as backup or supplement.

Critical
Fiber Connectivity

Multiple diverse fiber routes to the site. Low latency to major network exchange points. Presence of subsea cable landings or major network hubs nearby preferred for hyperscale.

Important
Natural Hazard Profile

Flood zone designation (avoid FEMA Zone AE), seismic risk, wildfire exposure, and severe weather history. 100-year flood and 500-year flood plain evaluation for both site and utility infrastructure.

Important
Land & Zoning

Sufficient acreage for planned campus including future expansion phases. Existing heavy industrial or data center zoning preferred. Assess entitlement timeline and community reception risk.

Important
Water Supply

Sufficient municipal or well water capacity for evaporative cooling. Some markets are restricting water use for data centers — assess regulatory environment and alternative cooling strategies.

Consider
Tax Incentives

Many states and localities offer data center tax exemptions on equipment and energy. Sales tax exemptions on IT equipment can represent significant capital savings on a large campus.

Entitlement & Permitting

Zoning & Land Use

Data centers in many jurisdictions require special use permits or conditional use approvals even in industrial zones. Community opposition around traffic, noise (cooling equipment), visual impact, water use, and tax abatements is increasingly common. Engage early with planning departments and adjacent property owners.

In markets where data center development is new, expect longer entitlement timelines — regulators and elected officials may not have existing frameworks and will take time to evaluate the project.

Building & Construction Permits

Large data center campuses typically require building permits, electrical permits, mechanical permits (HVAC, plumbing), fire suppression system permits, and potentially environmental permits depending on site conditions and cooling system design.

Permits for the utility interconnection and any on-site generation (generators, substations) may involve separate regulatory bodies from the local building department. Map all required permits and their issuing authorities at project kickoff.

Entitlement risk is real. Community opposition has killed or significantly delayed data center projects in Virginia, Nevada, and other major markets. Budget for community engagement, government affairs support, and entitlement timeline risk in your development schedule.
Section 03 — Execution
Project Phases
Full lifecycle execution for a hyperscale or colocation data center campus from site control through commercial operation.
1
Site Control & Development
  • Execute option agreement or purchase and sale agreement on target site
  • File interconnection application with utility — this starts the clock on the longest lead item
  • Conduct Phase I and Phase II environmental site assessments
  • Commission geotechnical investigation — soil bearing capacity, groundwater depth, seismic conditions
  • Initiate zoning and entitlement process; engage government affairs and community relations support
  • Develop conceptual campus master plan — building layout, power delivery, cooling infrastructure, expansion phasing
  • Obtain investment committee or board approval to proceed with development capital
2
Design & Engineering
  • Engage architect and MEP engineers — develop schematic design, design development, and construction documents
  • Define power architecture — utility feed voltage, number of utility feeds, on-site substation design, generator configuration
  • Design cooling infrastructure — air-side economization, direct liquid cooling, cooling tower, chilled water systems per PUE target
  • Specify UPS systems, PDUs, and power distribution architecture to the rack level
  • Design fiber entry and MDA/IDA network infrastructure
  • Develop DCIM (Data Center Infrastructure Management) and BMS (Building Management System) architecture
  • Conduct commissioning plan development in parallel with design — not after construction
3
Procurement
  • Issue LNTP for transformers and switchgear — 52–78 week lead times are common; this is typically the critical path item
  • Procure emergency backup generators — 40–60 week lead times for large diesel gensets
  • Order UPS systems, PDUs, and power distribution equipment
  • Procure cooling plant equipment — chillers, cooling towers, CRAHs/CRACs, pumps
  • Structural steel for data hall superstructure — often prefabricated for speed
  • Negotiate utility interconnection agreement and construction agreement; confirm utility delivery date in writing
  • Issue RFPs for general contractor and MEP subcontractors
4
Construction
  • Civil earthwork — grading, underground utilities, storm drainage, paving
  • Structural steel erection and building envelope — roof, exterior wall, dock doors
  • Utility substation construction and commissioning (may be utility-owned or owner-constructed)
  • Generator yard construction — concrete pads, fuel systems, exhaust and enclosures
  • MEP rough-in — conduit, cable tray, HVAC ductwork, plumbing rough
  • Power distribution installation — switchgear, transformers, UPS, PDU
  • Cooling system installation — chiller plant, cooling towers, piping, CRAH units
  • Fire suppression, life safety, and security systems installation
  • Data hall fit-out — raised floor or slab, cable management, network infrastructure
5
Commissioning (Cx)
  • Component-level testing — test every UPS, PDU, transfer switch, CRAH, and sensor individually
  • Integrated system testing — confirm systems work together under normal and fault conditions
  • Generator failover testing — black start, load bank testing at 100% capacity
  • UPS bypass and maintenance bypass testing
  • Cooling system commissioning — balance airflow, verify redundancy failover
  • Fire suppression discharge testing and life safety system verification
  • Scenario-based commissioning — simulate utility failure, cooling failure, N+1 failure conditions
  • Achieve Uptime Institute or equivalent certification if required by tenant
6
Commercial Operations
  • Formal handover to operations team with completed commissioning documentation
  • Tenant access and IT equipment deployment begins per lease agreement
  • DCIM system live — real-time monitoring of power, cooling, and environmental parameters
  • Ongoing capacity management — track PUE, power utilization, and cooling headroom
  • Initiate next phase of campus expansion planning based on demand signals
Section 04 — Infrastructure
Power & Utilities
Power delivery, backup generation, cooling systems, and utility interconnection — the critical infrastructure that defines data center viability.
Transformers and switchgear are the long lead items that will make or break your schedule. In the current market, large power transformers have lead times of 52–78+ weeks. Order these before you have finalized construction documents. The cost of carrying a transformer you might not need is far less than missing a COD.

Power Delivery Architecture

Utility Feed Strategy

Tier III and Tier IV data centers require N+1 or 2N redundant utility feeds from geographically diverse substations or separate feeders from the same substation. Confirm with the utility that redundant paths are truly diverse — two feeds from the same transformer bank are not redundant in any meaningful sense.

Utility interconnection agreements define the terms of power delivery including voltage, capacity, reliability guarantees, curtailment provisions, and upgrade cost responsibility. Review these carefully — the utility's standard agreement is not designed to protect the data center operator.

Critical Equipment — Lead Times

52–78 weeks
Power Transformers

Large substation and distribution transformers. Single largest schedule risk item. Order with LNTP before final design. Consider ordering spares — transformer failures have caused multi-week outages at major facilities.

40–60 weeks
Emergency Generators

Large diesel gensets (1–3 MW each). Number of units depends on N+1 or 2N backup strategy. Fuel systems (day tanks, main storage) have separate lead times. Secure generator pad designs early to avoid civil rework.

30–52 weeks
Switchgear & MV Equipment

Medium voltage switchgear, automatic transfer switches (ATS), and associated protection relays. Often manufactured in the same supply chain as transformers — coordinate delivery sequencing carefully.

24–40 weeks
UPS Systems

Uninterruptible power supply systems for IT load protection. Lead time has extended significantly in recent years. Define UPS topology (double conversion, modular, etc.) early to lock in vendor and delivery slot.

20–36 weeks
Chillers & Cooling Towers

For chilled water cooling systems. Cooling tower lead times have also extended. Coordinate with mechanical engineer on sequence — chiller plant typically needs to be operational before data hall can accept IT load.

16–24 weeks
CRAH / CRAC Units

Computer Room Air Handlers / Air Conditioners for in-row or perimeter cooling. Lead times vary significantly by manufacturer and configuration. Order early relative to data hall construction completion.

Section 05 — Risk
Risk Register
Key development and execution risks specific to large-scale data center projects.
RiskCategoryLevelMitigation
Utility interconnection delay — grid capacity unavailable or queue delayed Schedule / Fatal High File interconnection application before land acquisition if possible. Pursue multiple sites simultaneously. Evaluate on-site generation as partial alternative.
Transformer / switchgear delivery delay Schedule High Issue LNTP immediately at project sanction. Order before final design is complete. Consider ordering spare transformers for large campuses.
Entitlement denied or significantly delayed by community opposition Schedule / Fatal High Early community engagement and government affairs strategy. Identify opposition risks during site selection. Have backup sites under evaluation in parallel.
Hyperscale tenant COD miss — customer cancels or seeks damages Commercial High Maintain detailed schedule with float tracking. Escalate schedule risks early. Build contractual flexibility into tenant agreements where possible.
Geotechnical surprises requiring foundation redesign Cost / Schedule Medium Thorough geotechnical investigation in development phase. Budget foundation contingency for brownfield or previously disturbed sites.
Water availability restrictions imposed during development Operational / Regulatory Medium Assess local water policy environment during site selection. Design flexibility for air-side economization to reduce water dependency.
Construction labor shortage in target market Schedule / Cost Medium Contract with GC early and confirm labor availability. Consider prefabricated building systems to reduce on-site labor requirements.
Commissioning failures requiring equipment rework Schedule / Cost Low Engage commissioning agent from design phase. Develop commissioning plan early. Do not compress commissioning schedule to recover construction delays.
Section 06 — Controls
Project Controls
Budget management, schedule control, and reporting framework for data center development execution.

Development Cost Categories

Land & Entitlement
Site Acquisition

Land purchase, option fees, due diligence costs, entitlement fees, legal, government affairs, community engagement. Typically 5–15% of total project cost depending on market.

Hard Costs
Construction

Civil, structural, MEP, fit-out, and utility construction. Largest cost category. Typically bid as GMP (Guaranteed Maximum Price) or design-build. Track committed vs. budget weekly during construction.

Soft Costs
Professional Services

Architecture, engineering, commissioning agent, legal, permitting fees, project management. Typically 8–15% of hard costs. Often underestimated in early pro formas.

Owner-Direct
Equipment

Transformers, generators, UPS, switchgear procured directly by owner. Tracked separately from GC contract. Requires dedicated procurement and expediting management.

A commissioning failure after tenant IT equipment is installed is catastrophically expensive — and reputationally damaging.
