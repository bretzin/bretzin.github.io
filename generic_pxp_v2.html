Project Purpose

This template establishes the execution framework for a capital infrastructure project. It covers the full lifecycle from early development through operations handover, providing a consistent reference for project team members, contractors, and stakeholders across disciplines.

Each section addresses a distinct execution domain — phases, procurement, controls, risk, and stakeholder management — with enough specificity to drive alignment without being prescriptive about project-specific details that belong in dedicated work plans.

Project Objectives

Primary
On-Time Delivery

Achieve mechanical completion and commissioning within the approved project schedule, meeting all contractual milestones and avoiding liquidated damages exposure.

Primary
Within Approved Budget

Complete the project within sanctioned capital, with committed cost tracking, change order discipline, and contingency management throughout execution.

Primary
Scope Integrity

Deliver all contracted scope to specification. Changes to scope must follow the formal change management process with impact assessment before approval.

Primary
Safety & Quality

Zero recordable safety incidents. All work performed to applicable codes, standards, and project specifications with documented QA/QC records.

Section 02 — Execution
Project Phases
End-to-end project lifecycle for a capital infrastructure project from development sanction through operations handover.
1
Development & Sanction
  • Define project scope, objectives, and success criteria; align with sponsor and stakeholders
  • Complete FEED (Front End Engineering & Design) — PFDs, P&IDs, equipment sizing, ±15% cost estimate
  • Obtain all required permits, land rights, and regulatory approvals
  • Develop project execution plan (PXP), contracting strategy, and procurement plan
  • Secure financing or capital approval; establish project controls baseline
  • Issue Limited Notice to Proceed (LNTP) on long lead equipment immediately after sanction
  • Execute Master Service Agreements with key contractors and vendors
2
Detailed Engineering
  • Complete detailed design across all disciplines — mechanical, civil, structural, electrical, instrumentation
  • Issue Issued for Construction (IFC) drawings and specifications to contractors
  • Finalize equipment datasheets and purchase specifications
  • Conduct HAZOP, SIL review, and safety instrumented system (SIS) design
  • Develop construction work packages and sequences
  • Complete constructability reviews with construction contractor
  • Finalize material take-offs and confirm procurement quantities
3
Procurement
  • Issue purchase orders for all long lead equipment — track against delivery commitments weekly
  • Conduct technical bid evaluations; award contracts to qualified vendors
  • Conduct Factory Acceptance Testing (FAT) on critical equipment before shipment
  • Manage logistics — shipping, customs, receiving, inspection, storage
  • Expedite all items on critical path; identify and mitigate delivery risks proactively
  • Maintain material tracking log — PO status, expected delivery, actual delivery, location
  • Manage vendor documentation — data books, as-built drawings, O&M manuals
4
Construction
  • Mobilize construction contractor; establish site safety plan and emergency response procedures
  • Execute civil earthwork, foundations, structural steel, equipment setting
  • Complete piping fabrication, installation, and NDE inspection
  • Complete electrical, instrumentation, and control system installation
  • Manage daily progress tracking, schedule updates, and weekly look-ahead planning
  • Issue RFIs and field change notices promptly; document all deviations from design
  • Conduct pre-commissioning punch list walks; close outstanding items before system handover
  • Coordinate tie-in outage windows with operations where applicable
5
Commissioning & Startup
  • Instrument calibration across all measurement devices
  • Control system loop checks and functional logic testing
  • Safety system testing — ESD logic, pressure relief, fire and gas response
  • Mechanical run test of rotating equipment
  • System performance testing against design basis
  • Operator training and handover of O&M manuals and as-built drawings
  • Regulatory notifications and final permits where required
6
Closeout
  • Finalize all punch list items and close outstanding RFIs and change orders
  • Compile and submit final as-built documentation package
  • Complete vendor warranty registrations and final invoicing
  • Conduct project lessons learned session with full team
  • Final cost reconciliation and budget closeout report
  • Formal handover to operations with signed mechanical completion certificate
Section 03 — Procurement
Procurement Strategy
Equipment categories, lead times, and procurement priorities across a typical capital project portfolio.
Long lead equipment governs the project schedule. The first procurement action after sanction should always be issuing LNTP or purchase orders for equipment with lead times exceeding 6 months. Delay here compresses every downstream phase.

Equipment Categories by Lead Time

12–24 months lead
Long Lead — Critical Path

Large rotating equipment (compressors, turbines, pumps), custom pressure vessels, heat exchangers, large transformers, switchgear, specialty valves. Order immediately after sanction. These govern the schedule.

6–12 months lead
Medium Lead — Monitor Closely

Control systems (DCS/PLC), packaged skid equipment, electrical MCC and distribution panels, specialty instruments, structural steel, bulk line pipe. Order early in detailed engineering phase.

4–8 weeks lead
Standard Bulk — Manage Quantities

Standard valves, pipe fittings, cable, conduit, instrumentation tubing, concrete and aggregate, fasteners. Manage through material take-offs and purchase early enough to avoid site delays.

Variable
Contractor-Furnished — Verify Early

Items furnished by the EPC or construction contractor. Confirm procurement status and delivery commitments in early contractor meetings — contractor supply chain problems become your schedule problems.

Procurement Best Practices

Bid Evaluation & Award

Use structured technical and commercial bid evaluations. Separate technical evaluation from commercial to avoid price anchoring. Require bidders to confirm delivery dates in writing — verbal commitments mean nothing.

Include lifecycle cost in major equipment evaluations, not just purchase price. A cheaper compressor with higher maintenance costs and longer downtime exposure may cost more over the project life.

Expediting & Delivery Management

Assign dedicated expediting coverage to all long lead items. Minimum monthly check-ins with vendors during manufacturing; weekly as delivery approaches. Visit the factory for critical equipment FAT.

Build delivery buffer into the project schedule for all critical equipment — assume at least one vendor will slip. The question is which one and by how much.

Documentation Requirements

Establish vendor documentation requirements at PO issuance. Final data books, as-built drawings, operating and maintenance manuals, spare parts lists, and warranty documentation must be received and reviewed before final payment. Don't close a PO without the complete documentation package.

Section 04 — Controls
Project Controls
Budget management, schedule control, change management, and reporting framework for capital project execution.

Cost Tracking Framework

Committed vs. Spent

Committed cost is the full value of a contract or purchase order from the moment it is executed — regardless of whether any work has been performed or invoiced. This is the number that matters for budget management. Once you sign the PO, the money is committed.

Spent cost (actuals) is what has been invoiced and paid to date. This lags committed cost, sometimes significantly. Never manage a project budget against actuals alone — you will not see overruns until it is too late to act.

Estimate to Complete (ETC) is the forecast of remaining cost to finish the work. Updated monthly or when scope changes. ETC + Actuals = Estimate at Completion (EAC). Compare EAC to budget to understand true project financial position.

Change Management

Change Order Discipline

Every change to scope, schedule, or budget must go through a formal change management process before work begins. Changes executed without approval become disputes later. Establish a change log from day one and review it in every project meeting.

Assess all changes for schedule impact, not just cost. A "zero cost" change that adds two weeks to the critical path is not a zero-cost change — the extended supervision, contractor overhead, and delay risk has a real dollar value.

Section 05 — Risk
Risk Register
Common risk categories across capital infrastructure projects — likelihood, impact, and mitigation strategies.
Risk management is not a one-time exercise. The risk register should be reviewed and updated at every project meeting. Risks change as the project progresses — some close, new ones emerge.
RiskCategoryLevelMitigation
Long lead equipment delivery delay Schedule High Issue LNTP immediately post-sanction. Weekly expediting. Build schedule float on critical equipment.
Scope creep from engineering changes Cost / Schedule High Strict change management process. No work outside contract without approved change order.
Permit delays from regulatory agencies Schedule High File permits as early as possible. Maintain active dialogue with agencies. Build schedule buffer around permit milestones.
Contractor resource availability Schedule Medium Contract early with preferred contractors. Confirm resource commitments at kickoff. Monitor crew levels weekly.
Material cost escalation Cost Medium Lock in prices early via purchase orders. Include escalation provisions in contracts for long-duration work.
Unforeseen site conditions Cost / Schedule Medium Thorough site investigation in early development. Maintain contingency for discovery items.
Key person turnover on project team Execution Medium Document all decisions and rationale. Cross-train team members. Avoid single points of knowledge failure.
Weather / force majeure delays Schedule Low Build weather contingency into schedule. Include appropriate force majeure provisions in contracts.
Section 06 — Stakeholders
Stakeholder Management
Stakeholder map, communication plan, and engagement strategy across the project lifecycle.

Internal Stakeholders

Sponsor / Owner
Executive Leadership

Provide capital approval, strategic direction, and final decision authority. Engage monthly via executive report. Escalate issues requiring decisions above PM authority promptly.

Technical
Engineering Team

Produce and own technical deliverables. Embedded in project from FEED through commissioning. Resolve technical queries from construction and vendors.

Finance
Finance & Accounting

Track committed and spent costs, process invoices, manage capital appropriations. Provide monthly cost reports. Coordinate with PM on change orders and budget adjustments.

Operations
Operations Team

End users of the completed facility. Engage throughout construction on operability and maintainability. Critical for coordinating tie-in outages and commissioning activities.

External Stakeholders

Contractors
EPC / Construction

Execute engineering, procurement, and construction scope under contract. Weekly progress meetings. Manage through clear scope definition, performance metrics, and proactive issue resolution.

Vendors
Equipment Suppliers

Manufacture and deliver engineered equipment per PO terms. Manage through expediting, FAT attendance, and delivery milestone tracking. Hold vendors to documentation commitments.

Regulatory
Permitting Agencies

Issue permits and conduct inspections. Engage proactively and early. Provide complete, accurate applications. Maintain a single point of contact for each agency relationship.

Community
Local Stakeholders

Adjacent landowners, community groups, local government. Proactive engagement reduces delay risk. Address concerns early before they escalate into project obstacles.
